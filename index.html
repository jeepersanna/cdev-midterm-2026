memory, music, and the making of a mind

This project is framed as a mixtape. Music was the most reliable constant of my upbringing — sometimes the only thing that felt entirely mine. Each section opens with a song chosen as an epigraph, not as decoration, but because some things resist being said plainly. The first songs are ones my father loved. They drifted from the record player through all of my childhood, including the hours I spent at the kitchen table doing schoolwork. As the playlist moves forward, so does the internal dialogue I carried through adolescence — the slow accumulation of questions I didn't yet have language for. I've included photographs of myself at different stages of my education as a reminder that underneath the theory and the retrospect is simply a kid trying to find her way.

01 — STRUCTURE BEFORE SELF

AN ANSWER KEY DETERMINED TRUTH.

K–12 entirely homeschooled
0 classroom peers
∞ unanswered questions

I began my formal education not in a classroom but at a kitchen table in Hamilton, Ohio, with packets mailed from a correspondence school in Baltimore. The school provided textbooks, a structured curriculum, and a remote teacher to grade my work. It was, in many ways, a rigorous classical education — orderly, sequential, and placed a high focus on testing.

I was my parents' oldest child and their first "experiment" in homeschooling, a role that came with unspoken expectations. My parents were evangelical Christians and conservative libertarians who believed, with deep conviction, that educating their children was both their moral right and their spiritual duty. The government had no place in that formation. Neither did public school. So I learned at home, and I learned early that having the "right" answer was incredibly important for the approval of my parents, and God.

The correspondence model rewarded precision. An answer key determined truth. One wrong answer felt like a failure — not just academic, but personal. Without peers to measure myself against, without the natural reassurance that other children also struggle, the answer key became my only mirror. I internalized its judgments completely. In the Bible, one of the ten commandments is to "honor thy father and mother." Being wrong meant I had also offended God.

Looking back through the lens of educational theory, what was missing in those early years becomes clear. Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development holds that children construct knowledge actively, through interaction with their environment and the people in it (Piaget, 1952). But the correspondence model gave me knowledge as a finished product — pre-assembled, authoritative, and delivered by mail. There was no room for the kind of exploratory, hands-on learning Piaget described as essential to intellectual growth. I was not constructing understanding; I was receiving it.

Even more significantly, Lev Vygotsky's concept of the Zone of Proximal Development — the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can achieve with guidance — was left largely unoccupied (Vygotsky, 1978). Vygotsky argued that learning is most powerful when a more knowledgeable other (a teacher, a peer, a mentor) scaffolds a student just beyond their current capacity. My only "more knowledgeable other" was the answer key. If I got it wrong, I had failed. My mom would say: let's figure out why, let's try again together. And while she had the best intentions, she was not trained in teaching practices. Additionally, the dynamic was complicated and contentious because she wasn't only my teacher, she was also my mother. The stakes were high for both of us, and that contention interfered with my ability to learn.

Learning is most powerful when a more knowledgeable other scaffolds a student just beyond their current capacity — not delivering knowledge as a finished product, but building it together.

— Vygotsky, 1978; Piaget, 1952

02 — THE LONELINESS OF LEARNING ALONE

MY HOMESCHOOL EXPERIENCE: LONELY.

My two younger brothers were also homeschooled, but they were not my peers in the intellectual sense — we were not grappling with the same material, debating the same ideas, or struggling through the same problems together. For a brief period, we attended cooperative learning groups with other homeschool families, but my family's financial instability made it too expensive to participate. And so, for the most part, I learned on my own.

John Dewey's philosophy of education speaks directly to what this absence cost me. Dewey argued that learning is inherently social — that knowledge is not simply transmitted from teacher to student, but constructed through dialogue, collaboration, and shared inquiry (Dewey, 1916). As McLeod (2025) articulates in his synthesis of Dewey's thought, learning is both an individual and a collaborative endeavor. It is through the friction of exchanging ideas, through having our assumptions challenged by others, and challenging theirs, that is when understanding deepens and learning can be cemented.

Constructivism also highlights the social aspect of knowledge construction. Learning is both an individual and a collaborative endeavor. By sharing ideas, learners can challenge each other's assumptions and collectively build deeper understanding.

— McLeod, 2025 (summarizing Dewey, 1916)

I had none of that friction. I existed within a self-reinforcing feedback loop. My curriculum presented its conclusions as absolute truth with a heavy religious bias. My parents, acting with genuine love and sincere conviction, affirmed those conclusions at home. There was no peer who might have said, that doesn't make sense to me, or I learned something different, or even simply why? Without diverse perspectives, my intellectual world was not expansive — it was intense and enclosed.

Constructivist theory, particularly as developed by Vygotsky (1978) and elaborated by later scholars, emphasizes that the social negotiation of meaning is not a supplement to learning but its very engine. When we explain our thinking to others, we are forced to organize it. When others push back, we are forced to refine it. When we hear different interpretations of the same material, we are forced to contend with complexity. I was denied all of this. My education encouraged me to think for myself, but it defined the boundaries of legitimate thought so narrowly, and surrounded me with so few other minds, that "thinking for myself" could only ever mean reaching the same conclusions.

03 — A BIBLICAL WORLDVIEW

THE HARM WAS NOT ONLY FALSE ACADEMICS. IT WAS STRUCTURAL.

black + white thinking
sacred vs. secular
Gee's Discourse

By fourth grade, the correspondence school had proven too rigid for me and my younger brother, and my mother made the shift to building her own curriculum. This is when our education became explicitly theological. For science, she chose Apologia — a curriculum whose mission statement leaves no ambiguity: "We believe that the Bible teaches that God created the heavens and the earth out of nothing in six days. We conclude that scientific evidence points to the earth being young."

With Apologia, my science education was not really science education. It was apologetics — the practice of defending a predetermined theological conclusion against all evidence to the contrary. The Big Bang Theory was not a framework to be examined but an absolute falsehood to be debunked. The story of Noah's Ark was not a biblical myth to be interpreted but a geological event to be proven using pseudoscience. Nearly a decade passed before I understood that what I'd been taught wasn't really science — it was religious belief dressed in scientific language.

But the harm was not only being taught false information. It was structural — it shaped how I thought, not just what I thought. The result was a profoundly binary cognitive framework: sacred versus secular, truth versus deception, God's universe versus the "unsaved" world. This kind of thinking is well-described in developmental psychology as black-and-white or dichotomous thinking — a mode of reasoning that categorizes experience in absolutes and resists nuance or ambiguity. In children, some degree of this is developmentally normal. But an educational environment that actively cultivates and rewards it can cement what should be a passing stage into a lasting cognitive pattern. I have done significant work over the years to recognize and interrupt this pattern in myself, but it has effected my education, my mental health, and my relationships.

James Paul Gee's theory of Discourse is a helpful framework for me to understand what was happening from an educational point of view (Gee, 1989). Gee distinguishes between primary Discourses — the language, values, and ways of being we acquire in our early home environments — and secondary Discourses, the broader social and institutional identities we develop through school, work, and community participation. For me, evangelical Christianity and homeschooling were fused into a single, all-encompassing primary Discourse. They did not merely describe who I was at home; they defined who I was, period.

The result was a profoundly binary cognitive framework: sacred versus secular, truth versus deception, God's universe versus the "unsaved" world.

04 — UNSCHOOLING AND THE WEIGHT OF TOTAL FREEDOM

THE WEIGHT OF FREEDOM.

NO CURRICULA self-directed
NO SUPPORT no feedback
NO ROADMAP no future imagined

By high school, my mother had cycled through several more curriculums — Ambleside Online, Charlotte Mason, various free digital resources — before arriving at unschooling. It is the most radical form of child-led, self-directed learning: no fixed curriculum, no mandatory assignments, no conventional testing. The child follows their own curiosity, on their own terms.

In theory, unschooling is a liberating model. And there were moments that felt genuinely free — reading whatever I wanted, following my curiosity wherever it led, writing papers on Lord of the Rings, teaching myself Tolkien's elvish, spending long hours with the history and geography I loved. But freedom is only liberating if you have the tools to use it. Zaretta Hammond (2015) argues that the goal of education is not simply to fill students with information, but to help them 'learn how to learn' (p. 1). Without that foundation, she writes, a dependent learner cannot perform complex tasks 'without continuous support' (Hammond, 2015, p. 29-30). I was not yet a self-directed learner. My entire way of thinking had been shaped by a single Primary Discourse — what James Paul Gee (1989) describes as the language, values, and behaviors absorbed through home and family from birth, functioning as an 'identity kit' (p. 18). Gee (1989) observes that 'school-based secondary discourses conflict with the values and viewpoints' of children whose primary discourse is rooted in religious community (p. 24). I had not developed the Secondary Discourses that would allow me to move through new intellectual territory on my own terms. Unschooling assumed exactly the kind of learner I had not yet become, and without structure or scaffolding, the freedom didn't open things up — it left me adrift.

Here is the problem. On his own, a dependent learner is not able to do complex, school-oriented learning tasks such as synthesizing and analyzing informational text without continuous support.

— Zaretta Hammond, Culturally Responsive Teaching and the Brain, 2015

Gee (1989) describes a Discourse as an 'identity kit' — a complete package of ways of thinking, speaking, and acting that allows one to take on a recognizable role within a social group (p. 18). These kits are not developed in isolation; they require access to institutions and the communities within them. I did have influential secondary institutions — primarily church and youth group — but these were extensions of my Primary Discourse rather than departures from it. They reinforced the same values, the same ways of interpretation, the same worldview. It was music that first offered me something different. As a teenager I began developing my own musical taste, independent of my father's record player, and music became its own kind of identity kit — a space where I could try on new ways of feeling and being without having to explain or justify them. It also brought me into relationship with different kinds of people and ways of thinking. Gee (1989) notes that discourses 'can call for one to accept values in conflict with other discourses one is a member of' (p. 19). Music was one of the places I felt that conflict first — and where I began to have language for a confusion that had no clear place to go.

As I neared 12th grade graduation, that confusion came to a head. I was paralyzed. I had been told my whole life that my purpose was to be a faithful Christian — to serve God, to serve my future husband, perhaps to go into mission work. Careers were barely discussed. College was an afterthought. And when the world suddenly seemed to require that I have a direction, I had almost no framework for imagining one. The anxiety I felt was not laziness or lack of ambition. It was the logical consequence of an education that had never asked me to imagine a future for myself beyond the walls of my faith.

05 — WHAT WAS MISSING

"WHAT MY EXPERIENCE ILLUMINATES IS NOT THE FAILURE OF INDIVIDUAL PEOPLE."

Gee 1989
Dewey 1916
Piaget 1952
Vygotsky 1978
Ryan & Deci 2000

What my education made clear — in ways I am still tracing — is how completely a structure can shape what feels thinkable. Homeschooling is not inherently the problem. Neither are the people who choose it. The problem is the frameworks people carry into it — the ideological, religious, and political assumptions that determine what gets taught, what gets excluded, and whose version of knowledge counts as truth. Those frameworks are rarely chosen consciously. They are inherited, absorbed, passed down through exactly the kind of primary Discourses Gee (1989) describes — identities so total that the very act of questioning them can feel like self-betrayal.

And Dewey (1916), Piaget (1952), and Vygotsky (1978) each, in different ways, describe what those frameworks crowded out: the friction of other minds, the scaffolding of a knowledgeable guide, the understanding that knowledge is built in relationship — not received in isolation. I had the isolation. I was missing everything else.

Educational equity is a matter of asking who gets to shape what is taught, in whose image learning is designed, and which students are permitted to see themselves as full participants in the construction of knowledge.

— Gee, 1989; Dewey, 1916

The loneliness I felt as a student has become something I carry into every classroom I enter. I know what it feels like to be on the outside of a conversation everyone else seems fluent in. To carry an education that prepared you for one world and dropped you into another. To feel like the rules of learning were written for someone else. That knowledge doesn't make me a better teacher by itself — but it makes me a more clear-eyed one. The kind who knows that good intentions inside a broken framework are still a broken framework. The kind who will look for what the framework is doing to the child, not just what the teacher is trying to do for them.

06 — AND WHAT REMAINS

"THESE ARE NOT ONLY PEDAGOGICAL QUESTIONS. THEY ARE POLITICAL ONES."

EMPATHY the engine
EQUITY the goal
ADVOCACY the work

I used to think the gaps in my education were mine to overcome — deficits to quietly correct before anyone noticed. It took a long time to understand that they weren't personal failures. They were the logical outcome of a structure that was never designed to leave room for doubt, or divergence, or a self that didn't fit the plan.

We know that the best and most organic learning builds on what students already know. We need to see students — and have them see themselves — as capable learners who can learn and do anything.

— Diana Turk, NYU Steinhardt, 2023

Diana Turk's framing stayed with me: that we need to see students — and have them see themselves — as capable learners. I spent years not seeing myself that way. Not because I lacked ability, but because no one had ever framed my learning as something I was building. It was something being done to me, or for me, or through me — but rarely with me.

That is the thing I most want to change. Not to offer a corrected version of my own education, but something genuinely different — a classroom where a student's confusion is the starting point, not a problem to be managed. I've spent enough time on the outside of that kind of learning to know exactly what it looks like from in here.

// FIN

EDUCATION MUST CENTER POSSIBILITY, COMMUNITY, AND COLLECTIVE RESPONSIBILITY.

What I know now is that education is never neutral. Every curriculum makes a claim about who matters, what counts as knowledge, and whose future is worth imagining. I grew up inside a system that answered those questions very narrowly. I am still, in some ways, learning my way out. But I am also here — in graduate school, writing this, thinking through the structures that shaped me with tools I wasn't given at that kitchen table in Ohio. The questions I'm asking now are ones I didn't know I was allowed to ask then. That feels like something worth naming. I started this project with a mixtape because music was the first place the framework cracked — the first space where I encountered a self that didn't have to be explained or justified. I think that is, in the end, what good education does. It creates the conditions for a person to become someone they couldn't have predicted. I didn't get that early enough. But I know what it sounds like. And I know how to make room for it.
